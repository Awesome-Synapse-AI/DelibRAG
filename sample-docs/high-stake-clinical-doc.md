## 📋 Normal Adult Vital Sign Ranges (Baseline Reference)

| Vital Sign | Normal Range |
|------------|--------------|
| Heart Rate (HR) | 60-100 bpm |
| Respiratory Rate (RR) | 12-20 breaths/min |
| Blood Pressure (BP) | 90-120/60-80 mmHg |
| Temperature | 36.1-37.2°C (97-99°F) |
| Oxygen Saturation (SpO2) | 95-100% |
| Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) | 70-100 mmHg |

## 🫀 CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASES

### ACUTE MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION (Heart Attack)

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Systolic BP | <90 mmHg | Cardiogenic shock risk |
| Systolic BP | >180 mmHg | Increased cardiac workload |
| Heart Rate | <50 bpm | Heart block/bradycardia |
| Heart Rate | >120 bpm | Tachycardia/arrhythmia |
| SpO2 | <94% | Hypoxemia |

#### Management Protocols

**For Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg):**
1. **Immediate assessment:**
   - Check for signs of shock (cool extremities, altered mental status, decreased urine output)
   - Assess for right ventricular infarction (check right-sided leads)
   - Review medications (hold ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, nitrates)

2. **Interventions:**
   - Place patient in Trendelenburg position (if no pulmonary edema)
   - Establish IV access if not already present
   - Administer IV fluid bolus 250-500 mL normal saline (ONLY if no signs of heart failure)
   - **AVOID fluids if JVD, crackles, or S3 gallop present**
   - Notify physician immediately for vasopressor orders if fluid refractory
   - Prepare for possible emergent cardiac catheterization

3. **Continuous monitoring:**
   - BP every 5-15 minutes
   - Cardiac monitor for arrhythmias
   - Urine output

**For Hypertension (SBP >180 mmHg):**
1. **Assessment:**
   - Reassess pain level (uncontrolled pain raises BP)
   - Check for signs of pulmonary edema
   - Assess for aortic dissection (unequal pulses, tearing back pain)

2. **Interventions:**
   - Optimize pain control (morphine 2-4 mg IV if appropriate)
   - Administer sublingual nitroglycerin 0.4 mg (if SBP >90 mmHg)
   - May repeat nitroglycerin every 5 min × 3 doses
   - Consider IV nitroglycerin infusion per protocol
   - IV beta-blocker (metoprolol 5 mg) if appropriate and no contraindications

3. **Contraindications to be aware of:**
   - DO NOT give nitrates if phosphodiesterase inhibitor use in last 24-48 hours
   - DO NOT give beta-blockers in cocaine-induced MI

**For Bradycardia (HR <50 bpm):**
1. **Assessment:**
   - Determine if patient is symptomatic (chest pain, hypotension, altered mental status, dyspnea)
   - Identify type: sinus bradycardia vs. heart block
   - Review medications (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin)

2. **If symptomatic:**
   - Atropine 0.5 mg IV, may repeat every 3-5 min (max 3 mg)
   - If refractory: transcutaneous pacing
   - Prepare for transvenous pacing
   - Consider dopamine or epinephrine infusion

3. **If asymptomatic:**
   - Continue monitoring
   - Hold bradycardic medications
   - Document and notify physician

**For Tachycardia (HR >120 bpm):**
1. **Identify rhythm:**
   - Obtain 12-lead ECG
   - Distinguish sinus tachycardia vs. arrhythmia (VT, VF, AFib, AFlutter)

2. **If sinus tachycardia:**
   - Treat underlying cause (pain, anxiety, hypovolemia, heart failure)
   - Optimize pain management
   - Consider beta-blocker if no contraindications

3. **If ventricular tachycardia:**
   - **If unstable:** Immediate synchronized cardioversion
   - **If stable:** Amiodarone 150 mg IV over 10 min
   - Have defibrillator immediately available

4. **If atrial fibrillation with RVR:**
   - Rate control: metoprolol or diltiazem per protocol
   - Assess for anticoagulation needs

**For Hypoxemia (SpO2 <94%):**
1. **Immediate interventions:**
   - Apply supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO2 ≥94%
   - Position patient at 30-45 degree angle
   - Assess lung sounds for pulmonary edema

2. **If pulmonary edema present:**
   - Administer diuretics (furosemide 40 mg IV)
   - Consider nitroglycerin
   - Prepare for possible BiPAP/CPAP
   - Notify physician for possible catheterization

### CONGESTIVE HEART FAILURE (CHF) Exacerbation

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Respiratory Rate | >20 breaths/min | Pulmonary congestion |
| SpO2 | <92% on room air | Inadequate oxygenation |
| Systolic BP | <90 mmHg | Cardiogenic shock |
| Heart Rate | >100 bpm at rest | Decompensation |
| Weight gain | >2 lbs/day or >5 lbs/week | Fluid overload |
| Urine output | <30 mL/hour | Renal hypoperfusion |

#### Management Protocols

**For Acute Decompensated Heart Failure:**

1. **Immediate assessment:**
   - Assess volume status: JVD, peripheral edema, lung sounds
   - Obtain daily weight comparison
   - Check BNP or NT-proBNP if available
   - Review input/output records

2. **Positioning:**
   - Elevate head of bed to 45-90 degrees
   - Allow legs to dangle to decrease preload

3. **Oxygen therapy:**
   - Apply oxygen to maintain SpO2 >92%
   - Consider high-flow nasal cannula or BiPAP if worsening

4. **Diuretic therapy:**
   - Administer IV loop diuretic (furosemide 40-80 mg IV, or 2.5× home dose)
   - Monitor urine output closely (goal >0.5 mL/kg/hr)
   - Expect diuresis within 1-2 hours
   - May require continuous infusion if refractory

5. **Vasodilator therapy (if SBP >90 mmHg):**
   - Nitroglycerin IV infusion starting at 10-20 mcg/min
   - Titrate to symptom relief and BP tolerance
   - Monitor BP every 15 minutes initially

6. **If hypotensive (SBP <90 mmHg):**
   - **AVOID IV fluids** (will worsen heart failure)
   - Hold diuretics and vasodilators temporarily
   - Consider inotropic support (dobutamine, milrinone)
   - Urgent cardiology consultation
   - May need invasive hemodynamic monitoring

7. **Monitoring:**
   - Continuous pulse oximetry
   - Vital signs every 15-30 minutes initially
   - Strict intake and output
   - Daily weights (same scale, same time)
   - Electrolytes (especially potassium, magnesium with diuresis)
   - Renal function

8. **Escalation criteria:**
   - Worsening respiratory distress
   - Decreasing SpO2 despite oxygen
   - Altered mental status
   - Decreased urine output despite diuretics
   - **→ Consider ICU transfer**

### HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| BP | ≥180/120 mmHg + end-organ damage | Emergency |
| BP | ≥180/120 mmHg without symptoms | Urgency |

#### Management Protocols

**Hypertensive Emergency (with end-organ damage):**

**Signs of end-organ damage:**
- Encephalopathy (confusion, headache, vision changes, seizures)
- Acute stroke
- Acute MI or unstable angina
- Acute pulmonary edema
- Acute kidney injury
- Aortic dissection
- Eclampsia

**Management:**
1. **Initial assessment:**
   - Obtain accurate BP in both arms
   - Neurological exam
   - Cardiovascular exam (murmurs, pulmonary edema)
   - Fundoscopic exam if able (papilledema)

2. **Monitoring setup:**
   - Continuous cardiac monitoring
   - Arterial line placement for continuous BP monitoring (preferred)
   - ICU-level care

3. **BP reduction goals:**
   - **General rule:** Reduce MAP by 10-20% in first hour
   - **DO NOT drop too quickly** (risk of stroke, MI, renal failure)
   - Further reduce to 160/100 mmHg over next 2-6 hours
   - Normalize over 24-48 hours

4. **IV antihypertensive agents (choose based on clinical scenario):**

   **Nicardipine (preferred for most situations):**
   - Start 5 mg/hr, titrate by 2.5 mg/hr every 5-15 min (max 15 mg/hr)
   - Easy to titrate, predictable

   **Labetalol:**
   - Bolus: 20 mg IV over 2 min, then 40-80 mg every 10 min (max 300 mg)
   - OR infusion: 0.5-2 mg/min
   - **Avoid in:** Asthma, heart block, cocaine use

   **Nitroprusside:**
   - Start 0.3 mcg/kg/min, titrate every 5 min
   - Requires arterial line monitoring
   - **Avoid prolonged use** (cyanide toxicity)
   - Good for aortic dissection (combine with beta-blocker)

   **Enalaprilat (for heart failure):**
   - 1.25 mg IV over 5 minutes, may repeat
   - Slower onset (15-30 min)

5. **Special considerations:**

   **Aortic dissection:**
   - **Aggressive BP control:** Target SBP 100-120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm
   - Beta-blocker FIRST (esmolol or labetalol), then vasodilator
   - Emergent CT or TEE
   - Emergent cardiothoracic surgery consult

   **Acute ischemic stroke:**
   - **DO NOT lower BP** unless >220/120 mmHg
   - If tPA candidate: must be <185/110 mmHg before treatment
   - Stroke team consultation

   **Eclampsia:**
   - Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis (4-6 g loading dose)
   - Labetalol or hydralazine for BP control
   - Prepare for emergent delivery

**Hypertensive Urgency (no end-organ damage):**
1. **Assess for secondary causes:** pain, anxiety, medication non-compliance, bladder distention
2. **Oral antihypertensives** (NOT sublingual - too unpredictable):
   - Captopril 25 mg PO
   - Clonidine 0.1-0.2 mg PO
   - Labetalol 200-400 mg PO
3. **Observation period:** 4-6 hours with BP rechecks
4. **Outpatient follow-up** within 24-72 hours
5. **Patient education** on medication compliance

## 🫁 RESPIRATORY DISEASES

### CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE (COPD) Exacerbation

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Respiratory Rate | >24 breaths/min | Increased work of breathing |
| SpO2 | <88% | Hypoxemia (target 88-92% in COPD) |
| Heart Rate | >110 bpm | Respiratory distress |
| Mental status | Confusion/drowsiness | Hypercapnia/hypoxemia |
| Accessory muscle use | Present | Severe distress |

#### Management Protocols

**Acute COPD Exacerbation:**

1. **Immediate assessment:**
   - Assess severity: ability to speak, accessory muscle use, mental status
   - Obtain brief history: usual baseline, triggers, home medications
   - Lung auscultation: wheezing, decreased breath sounds
   - **Obtain ABG** if moderate-severe (assess for CO2 retention)

2. **Oxygen therapy - CONTROLLED:**
   - **Target SpO2: 88-92%** (NOT higher - risk of CO2 retention)
   - Start with 1-2 L/min nasal cannula or 24-28% Venturi mask
   - Titrate slowly to target
   - Monitor closely for decreased respiratory drive
   - Repeat ABG 30-60 min after oxygen change

3. **Bronchodilator therapy:**
   - **Albuterol** 2.5-5 mg via nebulizer every 20 minutes × 3 doses, then every 1-4 hours
   - **Ipratropium** 0.5 mg via nebulizer combined with albuterol
   - May use continuous nebulization if severe

4. **Corticosteroids:**
   - **Prednisone 40 mg PO** daily × 5 days
   - OR **Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV** if unable to take PO

5. **Antibiotics (if indicated):**
   - If purulent sputum AND increased dyspnea/sputum volume
   - Azithromycin, doxycycline, or amoxicillin-clavulanate

6. **Monitoring for respiratory failure:**

   **Warning signs:**
   - Worsening mental status (somnolence, confusion)
   - Paradoxical breathing
   - Inability to speak in words
   - SpO2 declining despite oxygen
   - pH <7.30 with PaCO2 >50 mmHg

   **If impending respiratory failure:**
   - **Non-invasive ventilation (NIV/BiPAP)** - first-line if alert and cooperative
     - Settings typically: IPAP 10-12, EPAP 4-5, titrate as tolerated
     - Reduces intubation rates by 50%
     - **Contraindications:** Unable to protect airway, hemodynamic instability, recent facial surgery
   
   - **Intubation and mechanical ventilation** if:
     - Unable to tolerate NIV
     - Respiratory arrest
     - Severe acidosis (pH <7.25)
     - Altered mental status preventing NIV

7. **Ventilator settings (if intubated):**
   - Low tidal volumes (6-8 mL/kg ideal body weight)
   - Longer expiratory time (I:E ratio 1:3 or 1:4)
   - Permissive hypercapnia may be acceptable
   - Monitor for auto-PEEP/dynamic hyperinflation

8. **Avoid:**
   - High-flow oxygen (target is 88-92%, not normal levels)
   - Sedatives (depress respiratory drive)
   - Beta-blockers (bronchospasm)

### ASTHMA - Severe Exacerbation

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Respiratory Rate | >30 breaths/min (adult) | Severe exacerbation |
| Heart Rate | >120 bpm | Severe distress |
| SpO2 | <90% | Life-threatening |
| Peak flow | <50% personal best | Severe obstruction |
| Speech | Single words only | Critical |
| Silent chest | Absent wheeze | Impending arrest |

#### Management Protocols

**Severe Asthma Exacerbation:**

1. **Rapid assessment:**
   - Ability to speak (sentences vs. words)
   - Peak expiratory flow if able (compare to personal best)
   - Listen for wheeze vs. silent chest (silent = critical)
   - Assess for altered mental status

2. **Immediate interventions:**

   **Oxygen:**
   - High-flow oxygen to maintain SpO2 >90% (no restriction like COPD)
   - Can use 100% oxygen if needed

   **Bronchodilators - AGGRESSIVE:**
   - **Albuterol** continuous nebulization (10-15 mg/hr)
   - OR albuterol 2.5-5 mg every 20 minutes × 3, then reassess
   - **Ipratropium** 0.5 mg combined with albuterol
   
   **Corticosteroids - EARLY:**
   - **Prednisone 40-60 mg PO** or **Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV**
   - Give within first hour (takes 4-6 hours for effect)

3. **If inadequate response after 1 hour:**

   **Magnesium sulfate:**
   - 2 grams IV over 20 minutes
   - Bronchodilator effect
   - Check magnesium level first if renal impairment

   **Epinephrine:**
   - Consider if life-threatening: 0.3-0.5 mg IM (1:1000)
   - May repeat every 20 minutes × 3

   **Terbutaline:**
   - 0.25 mg subcutaneous
   - Alternative to epinephrine

4. **ICU-level interventions (if critical):**

   **Heliox:**
   - Helium-oxygen mixture (reduces airway resistance)
   - May improve oxygen delivery while awaiting other therapies

   **Non-invasive ventilation:**
   - BiPAP may help in cooperative patients
   - Controversial - risk of delayed intubation

   **Intubation (last resort):**
   - **Indications:** Respiratory arrest, severe acidosis, exhaustion, altered mental status
   - **Use caution:** High risk of barotrauma, hypotension
   - Ketamine preferred for induction (bronchodilator properties)
   - Avoid neuromuscular blockade if possible (can't assess improvement)

5. **Life-threatening signs - prepare for crash intubation:**
   - Silent chest
   - Cyanosis
   - Poor respiratory effort
   - Bradycardia (ominous - pre-arrest)
   - Confusion/drowsiness
   - **Have code team available**

6. **Monitoring:**
   - Continuous pulse oximetry
   - Cardiac monitoring
   - Peak flow every 1-2 hours after initial treatment
   - Vital signs every 15-30 minutes initially
   - Consider ABG if severe (assess pH, CO2)

7. **Disposition criteria:**

   **Discharge if:**
   - SpO2 >90% on room air
   - Peak flow >70% predicted or personal best
   - Sustained response 60 min after last bronchodilator
   - Able to walk without dyspnea

   **Admit if:**
   - Inadequate response to treatment
   - Peak flow <40% after treatment
   - High-risk patient (prior ICU admission, recent hospitalization)
   - Poor access to follow-up

### PNEUMONIA - Severe

#### Critical Thresholds (CURB-65 Criteria)
| Parameter | Threshold | Points |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Confusion | New onset | 1 |
| Urea (BUN) | >20 mg/dL | 1 |
| Respiratory Rate | ≥30 breaths/min | 1 |
| Blood Pressure | SBP <90 or DBP ≤60 | 1 |
| Age | ≥65 years | 1 |

**Score ≥3: Consider ICU admission**

#### Additional Critical Parameters
| Parameter | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| SpO2 | <90% on room air |
| Multilobar involvement | Present |
| Temperature | <35°C or >40°C |

#### Management Protocols

**Severe Pneumonia:**

1. **Initial assessment:**
   - Calculate CURB-65 or PSI score
   - Obtain chest X-ray
   - Blood cultures × 2 (before antibiotics)
   - Sputum culture if able
   - CBC, CMP, lactate

2. **Oxygen therapy:**
   - Target SpO2 >90%
   - May require high-flow nasal cannula
   - Consider BiPAP if hypoxemic and alert

3. **Early antibiotics (within 1 hour for severe cases):**

   **Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP):**
   - **Ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV** PLUS **Azithromycin 500 mg IV**
   - OR **Ceftriaxone** PLUS **Levofloxacin 750 mg IV**
   - If MRSA risk: add **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV**
   - If Pseudomonas risk: **Piperacillin-tazobactam** or **Cefepime**

4. **Fluid resuscitation (if sepsis present):**
   - 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus if hypotensive or lactate >2 mmol/L
   - See sepsis section below for details

5. **Monitoring:**
   - Vital signs every 4 hours (more frequent if unstable)
   - Daily chest X-ray if ICU
   - Repeat lactate if initially elevated
   - Monitor for complications (empyema, effusion)

6. **Ventilatory support escalation:**
   - Room air → Nasal cannula → High-flow nasal cannula → BiPAP → Intubation
   - If PaO2/FiO2 ratio <200: Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
   - Consider lung-protective ventilation

## 🧠 NEUROLOGICAL DISEASES

### ACUTE ISCHEMIC STROKE

#### Critical Blood Pressure Thresholds

| Clinical Scenario | BP Management |
|-------------------|---------------|
| **tPA candidate (before treatment)** | Must be <185/110 mmHg |
| **Post-tPA (24 hours)** | Maintain <180/105 mmHg |
| **NOT tPA candidate** | Permissive hypertension <220/120 mmHg |
| **Thrombectomy candidate** | <185/110 mmHg |

#### Other Critical Parameters
| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|---------|
| Temperature | <37.5°C (treat fever) |
| Blood glucose | 140-180 mg/dL |
| SpO2 | >94% |

#### Management Protocols

**Hyperacute Stroke Management (<4.5 hours):**

1. **Time is brain - rapid assessment:**
   - Last known normal time
   - NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) score
   - CT head (rule out hemorrhage)
   - Blood glucose
   - BP in both arms
   - ECG (rule out AFib)

2. **Blood pressure management:**

   **If tPA candidate and BP >185/110 mmHg:**
   - **Labetalol 10-20 mg IV** over 1-2 minutes, may repeat once
   - OR **Nicardipine infusion** 5 mg/hr, titrate by 2.5 mg/hr every 5-15 min
   - Check BP every 15 minutes
   - **Goal:** <185/110 mmHg to give tPA safely

   **Post-tPA BP management (critical for 24 hours):**
   - Monitor BP every 15 minutes × 2 hours
   - Then every 30 minutes × 6 hours
   - Then hourly × 16 hours
   - **If BP >180/105 mmHg:**
     - Labetalol 10 mg IV, may repeat/double every 10 minutes (max 300 mg)
     - OR Nicardipine infusion 5-15 mg/hr
   - **If BP not controlled:** STOP tPA infusion, obtain STAT CT (rule out hemorrhage)

   **If NOT receiving tPA:**
   - **Permissive hypertension** - do NOT lower unless >220/120 mmHg
   - Brain autoregulation is impaired; BP helps perfuse penumbra
   - Exception: If BP >220/120 mmHg, reduce by 15% in first 24 hours
   - Labetalol or nicardipine in small increments

3. **Glucose management:**
   - **Hypoglycemia (<60 mg/dL):** Immediate treatment with D50
   - **Hyperglycemia (>180 mg/dL):** Insulin to maintain 140-180 mg/dL
   - Both hypo and hyperglycemia worsen stroke outcomes

4. **Temperature management:**
   - **Treat fever aggressively** (acetaminophen, cooling measures)
   - Every 1°C increase worsens outcomes
   - Target <37.5°C

5. **Oxygen:**
   - Supplemental oxygen only if SpO2 <94%
   - Avoid hyperoxia

6. **Positioning:**
   - Head of bed flat (0-30 degrees) to improve cerebral perfusion
   - Exception: If aspiration risk, elevate to 30 degrees

7. **NPO status:**
   - Nothing by mouth until swallow screen passed
   - High aspiration risk in acute stroke

8. **Monitoring:**
   - Neuro checks every 15 minutes during and 2 hours post-tPA
   - Then hourly × 24 hours
   - **Any decline in NIHSS:** STAT CT head (rule out hemorrhagic conversion)

9. **Complications to watch:**
   - **Hemorrhagic transformation** (most common in first 24-36 hours)
   - **Cerebral edema** (peaks day 3-5 in large strokes)
   - **Malignant MCA syndrome** - may need decompressive craniectomy

### HEMORRHAGIC STROKE (Intracerebral Hemorrhage)

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Target | Significance |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Systolic BP | 140-160 mmHg | Prevent hematoma expansion |
| ICP | <20 mmHg | If monitored |
| CPP | 60-70 mmHg | If ICP monitored |
| Temperature | <37.5°C | Fever worsens outcome |

#### Management Protocols

**Acute ICH Management:**

1. **Aggressive early BP control (CRITICAL):**
   - **Goal: SBP 140-160 mmHg within 1 hour**
   - Every 10 mmHg increase associated with hematoma expansion

   **IV antihypertensive agents:**
   - **Nicardipine infusion:** Start 5 mg/hr, titrate every 5 minutes
   - **Labetalol:** 10-20 mg IV every 10 minutes
   - **Clevidipine:** 1-2 mg/hr, double every 2-5 minutes
   - **Monitor BP every 5 minutes** until stable, then every 15 min

2. **Reversal of anticoagulation (URGENT):**

   **If on warfarin:**
   - **Vitamin K 10 mg IV** slow push
   - **4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC)** - dose based on INR and weight
   - **Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)** if PCC unavailable (takes longer)
   - Check INR every 2-4 hours until normalized

   **If on dabigatran:**
   - **Idarucizumab 5 g IV** (specific reversal agent)

   **If on rivaroxaban/apixaban:**
   - **Andexanet alfa** (if available)
   - OR **4-factor PCC**

   **If on heparin:**
   - **Protamine sulfate** 1 mg per 100 units heparin

   **If on antiplatelets:**
   - Consider **platelet transfusion** if severe bleeding
   - **Desmopressin (DDAVP)** 0.3 mcg/kg IV if uremic platelet dysfunction

3. **Prevent seizures:**
   - **Levetiracetam 1000 mg IV** loading dose if subcortical location
   - Continue prophylaxis × 1 month
   - Monitor for clinical seizures

4. **Manage increased ICP (if present):**

   **Signs of elevated ICP:**
   - Decreasing GCS
   - Cushing's triad (bradycardia, hypertension, irregular respirations)
   - Pupillary changes
   - Posturing

   **Interventions:**
   - Elevate head of bed 30 degrees (midline position)
   - **Hyperosmolar therapy:**
     - **Mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg IV** over 15-20 min (check serum osmolality)
     - OR **Hypertonic saline 3% 250 mL** IV bolus
   - Avoid hypotonic fluids
   - Maintain euvolemia with isotonic saline
   - Consider ICP monitor if GCS ≤8
   - **Neurosurgery consult** for possible evacuation

5. **Monitoring:**
   - Neuro checks every 1 hour minimum
   - BP every 15 minutes until stable, then hourly
   - Continuous cardiac monitoring
   - If ICP monitor: maintain CPP 60-70 mmHg (CPP = MAP - ICP)

6. **Surgical indications - URGENT neurosurgery consult if:**
   - Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm with neurological deterioration
   - Hydrocephalus with decreased consciousness
   - Lobar hemorrhage with deterioration
   - Young patient with significant hematoma

### TRAUMATIC BRAIN INJURY (TBI) - Severe

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Target | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| MAP | >80 mmHg | Maintain cerebral perfusion |
| CPP | 60-70 mmHg | Cerebral perfusion pressure |
| Systolic BP | Avoid <90 mmHg | Doubles mortality |
| SpO2 | >95% | Avoid hypoxia |
| PaCO2 | 35-40 mmHg | Normocarbia (avoid hypercarbia) |
| ICP | <20 mmHg | If monitored |
| Temperature | 36-37°C | Avoid hyperthermia |

#### Management Protocols

**Severe TBI (GCS ≤8):**

1. **ABCs - Prevent secondary injury:**

   **Airway:**
   - **Intubate if GCS ≤8** ("GCS of 8, intubate")
   - Inline cervical spine stabilization during intubation
   - Prevent hypoxia and aspiration

   **Breathing:**
   - **Target PaCO2 35-40 mmHg** (normocarbia)
   - **Avoid hyperventilation** unless acute herniation (temporarily to PaCO2 30-35)
   - Prolonged hyperventilation causes cerebral ischemia
   - **SpO2 >95%**

   **Circulation:**
   - **Maintain SBP >90 mmHg at minimum (>100 preferred)**
   - Hypotension is #1 preventable cause of secondary injury
   - Resuscitate with isotonic fluids (normal saline, LR)
   - Vasopressors if needed (norepinephrine, phenylephrine)
   - **Avoid hypotonic fluids** (worsen cerebral edema)

2. **ICP management:**

   **ICP monitoring indications:**
   - Severe TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal CT
   - OR severe TBI with normal CT if 2 of: age >40, SBP <90, posturing

   **If ICP >20 mmHg:**
   
   **Tier 1 interventions:**
   - **Head of bed 30 degrees**, midline neck position
   - **Adequate sedation and analgesia:**
     - Propofol infusion 20-50 mcg/kg/min
     - Fentanyl boluses/infusion
   - **Drain CSF** if ventriculostomy present (5-10 mL)
   - **Hyperosmolar therapy:**
     - **Mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg IV** (check osmolality, hold if >320 mOsm/kg)
     - OR **Hypertonic saline 3% 250 mL** IV over 15-30 min (can repeat)
     - 23.4% saline 30 mL IV for refractory ICP spikes
   - **Maintain euvolemia** (CVP 5-10)

   **Tier 2 interventions (if refractory):**
   - **Mild hyperventilation:** PaCO2 30-35 mmHg (temporary measure)
   - **Neuromuscular blockade:** Prevent shivering, coughing
   - **Barbiturate coma:** Pentobarbital loading dose 10 mg/kg over 30 min
   - **Decompressive craniectomy:** Neurosurgery decision

3. **Blood pressure management:**
   - **Goal:** CPP 60-70 mmHg (CPP = MAP - ICP)
   - If no ICP monitor: maintain MAP >80 mmHg
   - **Vasopressors if needed:**
     - Norepinephrine 0.05-0.5 mcg/kg/min
     - Phenylephrine if tachycardic

4. **Prevent secondary insults:**

   **Seizure prophylaxis:**
   - **Levetiracetam 1000 mg IV** loading dose
   - Continue × 7 days
   - Does NOT prevent late seizures, only early

   **Glycemic control:**
   - Target glucose 140-180 mg/dL
   - Avoid hypoglycemia (<80) and severe hyperglycemia (>200)

   **Temperature:**
   - Treat fever aggressively: acetaminophen, cooling blankets
   - Target normothermia 36-37°C
   - Each 1°C increase in temp worsens outcome

   **Nutrition:**
   - Early enteral nutrition (by day 5-7)
   - High protein needs (1.5-2 g/kg/day)

5. **Monitoring:**
   - Continuous ICP monitoring if indicated
   - Hourly neuro checks (GCS, pupils)
   - Continuous cardiac monitoring
   - Arterial line for continuous BP
   - Avoid unnecessary stimulation

6. **Neurosurgical emergencies - STAT neurosurgery:**
   - Epidural hematoma with mass effect
   - Acute subdural hematoma with shift >5mm or GCS decline
   - Depressed skull fracture
   - Signs of herniation

7. **Signs of herniation (EMERGENCY):**
   - Unilateral dilated pupil
   - Posturing
   - Cushing's triad
   - Bradycardia with hypertension
   - **Immediate management:**
     - **Hyperventilate to PaCO2 30-35 mmHg**
     - **Hypertonic saline 23.4% 30 mL** IV push OR **Mannitol 1 g/kg**
     - **Elevate head of bed 30 degrees**
     - **Emergent neurosurgery consult**
     - Prepare for OR

## 🩸 SEPSIS & SEPTIC SHOCK

#### Critical Thresholds (Sepsis-3 Criteria)

**qSOFA (quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment) - ≥2 suggests sepsis:**
| Parameter | Threshold | Points |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Altered mental status | GCS <15 | 1 |
| Systolic BP | ≤100 mmHg | 1 |
| Respiratory Rate | ≥22 breaths/min | 1 |

**Septic Shock:**
- MAP <65 mmHg despite fluid resuscitation
- Lactate >2 mmol/L
- Requiring vasopressors

#### Management Protocols (Surviving Sepsis Campaign)

**Hour-1 Bundle (formerly 3 and 6-hour bundles):**

1. **Measure lactate level:**
   - If >2 mmol/L, remeasure within 2-4 hours
   - Lactate clearance is a resuscitation goal

2. **Obtain blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics:**
   - At least 2 sets (aerobic and anaerobic)
   - From different sites
   - **DO NOT delay antibiotics >45 minutes** for cultures

3. **Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics:**
   - **Within 1 hour of sepsis recognition**
   - Empiric choices depend on source:

   **Unknown source:**
   - **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV** PLUS **Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV**
   - OR **Vancomycin** PLUS **Cefepime 2 g IV** OR **Meropenem 1 g IV**

   **Suspected pneumonia:**
   - **Vancomycin** PLUS **Cefepime** PLUS **Azithromycin 500 mg IV**

   **Suspected abdominal source:**
   - **Piperacillin-tazobactam** OR **Meropenem**

   **Neutropenic fever:**
   - **Cefepime** OR **Meropenem** (add vancomycin if line infection suspected)

   **Adjust based on local antibiogram and patient risk factors**

4. **Rapid fluid resuscitation:**
   
   **If hypotensive (MAP <65) OR lactate ≥4 mmol/L:**
   - **30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus** (e.g., 2-3 liters for 70 kg patient)
   - Use isotonic crystalloids: normal saline or lactated Ringer's
   - Administer rapidly (over 1-3 hours)

   **Reassess after 30 mL/kg:**
   - Check BP, heart rate, urine output, capillary refill
   - Assess for fluid overload: lung sounds, JVD, edema
   - Consider dynamic measures (passive leg raise, pulse pressure variation)
   - May need additional 500 mL boluses based on response

   **If signs of fluid overload:**
   - Crackles on lung exam
   - Worsening oxygenation
   - **Consider vasopressors rather than more fluid**

5. **Begin vasopressors if hypotensive during/after fluid resuscitation:**

   **Target MAP ≥65 mmHg**

   **First-line: Norepinephrine**
   - Start 0.05 mcg/kg/min (typically 5-10 mcg/min)
   - Titrate every 5-10 minutes
   - Typical range 0.05-0.5 mcg/kg/min
   - **Requires central line** (or large peripheral IV temporarily)

   **Second-line: Vasopressin**
   - Add if norepinephrine >0.25 mcg/kg/min
   - Fixed dose: 0.03-0.04 units/min
   - Helps reduce norepinephrine dose

   **Epinephrine:**
   - If inadequate response to norepinephrine + vasopressin
   - 0.05-0.5 mcg/kg/min

   **DO NOT use dopamine** (associated with increased arrhythmias)

   **Consider phenylephrine** if tachyarrhythmias present

6. **Additional monitoring and interventions:**

   **Central venous access:**
   - For vasopressor administration
   - Consider central venous pressure monitoring
   - ScvO2 monitoring in refractory shock

   **Arterial line:**
   - For continuous BP monitoring on vasopressors
   - Facilitate frequent ABG sampling

   **Urine output:**
   - Foley catheter
   - Target >0.5 mL/kg/hr

   **Serial lactate measurements:**
   - Every 2-4 hours until normalized
   - Lactate clearance ≥10% or normalization

7. **Source control:**
   - Identify and treat source of infection
   - **Drain abscesses** - IR or surgical drainage
   - **Remove infected devices** - catheters, hardware
   - **Surgical consultation** if indicated - cholecystitis, bowel perforation, necrotizing fasciitis

8. **Additional considerations:**

   **Corticosteroids (if refractory shock):**
   - **Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day** IV (divided or continuous)
   - If requiring escalating vasopressors despite fluid resuscitation
   - Controversial - consider in consultation with ICU team

   **Blood products:**
   - Transfuse RBCs if Hgb <7 g/dL (or <9 if MI/severe hypoxemia)
   - Platelet transfusion if <10,000 or <50,000 with bleeding
   - Correct coagulopathy if DIC present

   **Glycemic control:**
   - Target glucose 140-180 mg/dL
   - Insulin infusion if needed

   **DVT prophylaxis:**
   - Subcutaneous heparin or LMWH unless contraindicated

   **Stress ulcer prophylaxis:**
   - PPI or H2 blocker if risk factors

9. **Escalation:**
   - **ICU transfer** for:
     - Requiring vasopressors
     - Requiring mechanical ventilation
     - Lactate >4 mmol/L
     - Multi-organ dysfunction
   - Early critical care consultation

10. **Monitoring vital signs in sepsis:**

**Continuous monitoring required:**
- Cardiac telemetry
- Continuous pulse oximetry
- Arterial line BP if on vasopressors

**Vital sign frequency:**
- **Every 15 minutes** until stable
- **Every 30-60 minutes** on vasopressors once stabilized
- **Every 4 hours** if improving

**Key parameters:**
- MAP ≥65 mmHg
- Urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr
- Lactate trending down
- Improving mental status

## 🔥 METABOLIC & ENDOCRINE EMERGENCIES

### DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|-----------|------|----------|--------|
| Blood glucose | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL |
| pH | 7.25-7.30 | 7.0-7.24 | <7.0 |
| Serum HCO3 | 15-18 mEq/L | 10-15 mEq/L | <10 mEq/L |
| Anion gap | >10 | >12 | >12 |
| Mental status | Alert | Alert/drowsy | Stupor/coma |

#### Management Protocols

**DKA Management:**

1. **Initial assessment:**
   - Confirm diagnosis: hyperglycemia + anion gap acidosis + ketones
   - Identify precipitant: infection, MI, medication non-compliance, new diabetes
   - Check electrolytes, BUN, Cr, glucose, ABG/VBG, beta-hydroxybutyrate or urine ketones
   - ECG (check for MI, assess for hyperkalemia)

2. **Fluid resuscitation (CRITICAL - most important intervention):**

   **Initial fluid:**
   - **Normal saline 1-1.5 L over first hour** (15-20 mL/kg)
   - Typical deficit is 5-7 liters

   **Subsequent fluids:**
   - **If corrected sodium normal or high:**
     - 0.45% saline at 250-500 mL/hr
   - **If corrected sodium low:**
     - Continue 0.9% saline at 250-500 mL/hr
   
   **When glucose <250 mg/dL:**
   - **Switch to D5 0.45% saline** at 150-250 mL/hr
   - Continue until acidosis resolved
   - Prevents hypoglycemia while continuing insulin

3. **Insulin therapy:**

   **DO NOT start insulin until:**
   - Potassium >3.3 mEq/L (risk of life-threatening hypokalemia)
   - After starting IV fluids

   **Insulin protocol:**
   - **Regular insulin 0.1 units/kg IV bolus**
   - Then **0.1 units/kg/hr continuous IV infusion** (typically 5-10 units/hr)
   
   **Monitor glucose hourly:**
   - **Goal:** Decrease 50-75 mg/dL per hour
   - **If not decreasing adequately:** Double insulin infusion rate hourly until consistent decline
   
   **When glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL:**
   - **Decrease insulin to 0.02-0.05 units/kg/hr** (2-5 units/hr)
   - Add dextrose to IV fluids (D5 0.45% saline)
   - **Continue insulin until acidosis resolved** (pH >7.3, HCO3 >18, anion gap closed)

4. **Potassium replacement (CRITICAL):**

   **DKA causes total body potassium depletion despite normal/high initial levels**
   
   **If K <3.3 mEq/L:**
   - **HOLD insulin**
   - Replace potassium aggressively
   - Recheck K in 2 hours

   **If K 3.3-5.2 mEq/L:**
   - **Add 20-30 mEq KCl to each liter of IV fluid**
   - Goal: maintain K 4-5 mEq/L
   
   **If K >5.2 mEq/L:**
   - Do NOT give potassium
   - Recheck every 2 hours (will drop with insulin/fluids)

   **Monitor potassium every 2-4 hours**

5. **Bicarbonate therapy (CONTROVERSIAL):**
   
   **Generally NOT recommended**
   
   **Consider ONLY if:**
   - pH <6.9
   - Severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes
   - Cardiac instability

   **If given:**
   - NaHCO3 100 mEq in 400 mL sterile water + 20 mEq KCl
   - Infuse over 2 hours
   - Risk: paradoxical CNS acidosis, hypokalemia

6. **Monitoring:**

   **Vital signs every 1-2 hours initially**

   **Labs:**
   - Glucose: **hourly** (point-of-care)
   - Electrolytes, anion gap: **every 2-4 hours**
   - VBG: **every 2-4 hours** until resolution
   - Phosphate: baseline and recheck (replacement controversial)

   **Mental status:** Frequent neuro checks

7. **Resolution criteria (ALL must be met):**
   - Glucose <200 mg/dL
   - pH >7.3
   - Serum bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L
   - Anion gap ≤12
   - Patient able to eat

8. **Transition to subcutaneous insulin:**

   **Overlap required (prevent DKA recurrence):**
   - Give subcutaneous basal insulin (NPH or glargine) 2 hours before stopping IV insulin
   - OR give rapid-acting insulin with meal 1-2 hours before stopping IV insulin
   - Continue IV insulin for 1-2 hours after subcutaneous given

9. **Complications to monitor:**

   **Cerebral edema (pediatric emergency):**
   - Headache, altered mental status, bradycardia, vomiting
   - More common in children
   - Risk factors: rapid glucose decline, excessive fluids, severe acidosis
   - **Treatment:**
     - **Mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg IV** OR **hypertonic saline 3% 2.5-5 mL/kg**
     - Slow IV fluids
     - Neurosurgery consult

   **Hypoglycemia:**
   - From excessive insulin
   - Add/increase dextrose in fluids

   **Hypokalemia:**
   - Life-threatening arrhythmias
   - Aggressive replacement

   **Non-anion gap acidosis:**
   - After DKA resolution (hyperchloremic)
   - Self-limited

### HYPEROSMOLAR HYPERGLYCEMIC STATE (HHS)

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | HHS |
|-----------|-----|
| Glucose | >600 mg/dL (often >1000) |
| Serum osmolality | >320 mOsm/kg |
| pH | >7.30 (minimal acidosis) |
| Bicarbonate | >18 mEq/L |
| Mental status | Often altered |

#### Management Protocols

**Similar to DKA but key differences:**

1. **More aggressive fluid resuscitation:**
   - Typical deficit 8-10 liters (vs. 5-7 in DKA)
   - **NS 1-1.5 L in first hour**
   - Then 250-500 mL/hr
   - More gradual replacement (over 24-48 hours)

2. **Lower insulin doses:**
   - Often more insulin-sensitive
   - Start 0.05-0.1 units/kg/hr
   - **Glucose goal:** Decline 50-70 mg/dL per hour
   - **DO NOT correct glucose too rapidly** (risk of cerebral edema)

3. **Correct hypernatremia slowly:**
   - Risk of osmotic demyelination
   - Corrected sodium should not fall >10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours

4. **Monitor mental status closely:**
   - Altered mental status common
   - Improves with treatment
   - If not improving: consider stroke, infection

5. **Higher thrombosis risk:**
   - Consider heparin prophylaxis or treatment dose

### THYROID STORM

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Temperature | >40°C (104°F) |
| Heart rate | >140 bpm |
| Systolic BP | Often >140 with wide pulse pressure |
| CNS | Agitation, delirium, psychosis, seizures |

#### Management Protocols

**Thyroid Storm (Life-Threatening Emergency):**

1. **Immediate supportive care:**

   **Temperature control:**
   - **Acetaminophen 650-1000 mg** every 4-6 hours
   - **Cooling blankets**
   - Ice packs to groin, axilla
   - **AVOID aspirin** (displaces thyroid hormone from binding proteins)
   - Target <39°C

   **Beta-blockade (FIRST-LINE):**
   - **Propranolol 1-2 mg IV every 10-15 minutes** until HR controlled
   - Then **40-80 mg PO every 4-6 hours**
   - OR **Esmolol infusion** 50-100 mcg/kg/min if cannot take PO
   - Target HR <100 bpm
   - **Contraindications:** Severe heart failure, asthma
     - Use diltiazem instead if contraindicated

2. **Block thyroid hormone synthesis:**

   **Thionamides (give 1 hour BEFORE iodine):**
   - **Propylthiouracil (PTU) 500-1000 mg loading dose**
   - Then **250 mg every 4 hours** PO or NG
   - OR **Methimazole 20 mg every 4-6 hours**
   - PTU preferred (also blocks T4→T3 conversion)

3. **Block thyroid hormone release:**

   **Iodine (give 1 hour AFTER thionamide):**
   - **Potassium iodide (SSKI) 5 drops PO every 6 hours**
   - OR **Lugol's solution 10 drops PO every 8 hours**
   - OR **Sodium iodide 500 mg IV every 12 hours**

4. **Block T4→T3 conversion:**

   **Corticosteroids:**
   - **Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV every 8 hours**
   - OR **Dexamethasone 2 mg IV every 6 hours**
   - Also treats possible adrenal insufficiency

5. **Additional therapies:**

   **Cholestyramine:**
   - 4 g PO every 6 hours
   - Binds thyroid hormone in gut

   **Plasmapheresis or dialysis:**
   - If refractory to medical therapy
   - Removes circulating thyroid hormone

6. **Treat underlying precipitant:**
   - Infection (most common)
   - DKA
   - MI
   - Trauma, surgery
   - Medication non-compliance

7. **Supportive care:**

   **Fluids:**
   - Often hypovolemic from fever, vomiting, diarrhea
   - Aggressive IV hydration with D5NS

   **Nutrition:**
   - High-calorie diet (increased metabolic demand)
   - Multivitamins, especially thiamine

8. **Monitoring:**
   - Continuous cardiac monitoring
   - Vital signs every 15-30 minutes initially
   - Hourly once stabilizing
   - Free T4, T3, TSH (for diagnosis, not for trending acutely)
   - Electrolytes, glucose
   - Assess for heart failure, arrhythmias

9. **ICU-level care required:**
   - High mortality (10-30% even with treatment)
   - May need mechanical ventilation, vasopressors

## 🩸 HEMORRHAGIC EMERGENCIES

### MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE / HEMORRHAGIC SHOCK

#### Critical Thresholds
| Parameter | Class III Hemorrhage | Class IV Hemorrhage |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Blood loss | 30-40% (1500-2000 mL) | >40% (>2000 mL) |
| Heart rate | >120 bpm | >140 bpm |
| Systolic BP | 80-90 mmHg | <70 mmHg |
| Shock Index (HR/SBP) | 1.0-1.4 | >1.4 |
| Mental status | Anxious, confused | Lethargic |

#### Management Protocols

**Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP):**

1. **Activate MTP when:**
   - Suspected loss of 1 blood volume (5 L) in 24 hours
   - OR 50% blood volume in 3 hours
   - OR rate of bleeding 150 mL/min
   - OR Shock Index >1.0 with ongoing bleeding

2. **Immediate actions:**

   **Airway/Breathing:**
   - High-flow oxygen or intubate if needed
   - Maintain SpO2 >94%

   **Large-bore IV access:**
   - Two 16-gauge or larger IVs
   - OR rapid infusion catheter
   - Consider intraosseous if difficult access

   **Stop the bleeding (source control):**
   - **Direct pressure** for external bleeding
   - **Tourniquet** for extremity hemorrhage (note time applied)
   - **Pelvic binder** for pelvic fractures
   - **REBOA** (resuscitative endovascular balloon occlusion) for torso hemorrhage
   - **Emergent surgery** for internal bleeding

3. **Resuscitation strategy - Damage Control Resuscitation:**

   **Balanced blood product ratio (1:1:1):**
   - 1 unit **Packed RBCs**
   - 1 unit **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)**
   - 1 unit **Platelets** (or 1 apheresis unit per 6 RBC)

   **First MTP pack typically contains:**
   - 6 units PRBCs
   - 6 units FFP
   - 1 apheresis platelet unit

   **Transfuse rapidly:**
   - Use rapid infuser or pressure bags
   - Warm blood products (prevent hypothermia)

   **Permissive hypotension (until bleeding controlled):**
   - Target SBP 80-90 mmHg (MAP 60-65)
   - Avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation before hemostasis
   - **Exception:** Traumatic brain injury - maintain SBP >90 mmHg

4. **Hemostatic adjuncts:**

   **Tranexamic acid (TXA):**
   - **1 gram IV over 10 minutes**
   - Then **1 gram IV over 8 hours**
   - **Give within 3 hours of injury** (most benefit <1 hour)
   - Antifibrinolytic - prevents clot breakdown

   **Calcium:**
   - **Calcium chloride 1 gram IV** or **Calcium gluconate 3 grams IV**
   - Citrate in blood products binds calcium
   - Hypocalcemia impairs coagulation

   **Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC):**
   - If on warfarin or factor deficiency
   - Rapid reversal of coagulopathy

   **Cryoprecipitate:**
   - If fibrinogen <150 mg/dL
   - Contains fibrinogen, Factor VIII, vWF

5. **Prevent/treat the lethal triad:**

   **Hypothermia (<35°C):**
   - Worsens coagulopathy
   - **Warm all fluids**
   - Use warming blankets
   - Increase room temperature
   - Warm air devices (Bair Hugger)

   **Acidosis (pH <7.2):**
   - Impairs coagulation cascade
   - **Treat with hemostasis and perfusion** (not bicarbonate)
   - Adequate ventilation

   **Coagulopathy:**
   - From dilution, consumption, hypothermia, acidosis
   - Replace factors with FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate
   - Goal INR <1.5, Platelets >50,000, Fibrinogen >150 mg/dL

6. **Laboratory monitoring (every 30-60 min during active resuscitation):**
   - **CBC** (hemoglobin, platelets)
   - **PT/INR, aPTT**
   - **Fibrinogen**
   - **Arterial blood gas** (pH, lactate, base deficit)
   - **Ionized calcium**
   - **TEG/ROTEM** (viscoelastic testing) if available

7. **Hemoglobin targets:**
   - **General:** Hgb >7 g/dL
   - **Active hemorrhage:** Hgb 7-9 g/dL
   - **Traumatic brain injury or cardiac disease:** Consider Hgb >10 g/dL

8. **Endpoints of resuscitation:**
   - Hemostasis achieved
   - Normal vital signs
   - Adequate urine output (>0.5 mL/kg/hr)
   - Lactate normalizing (<2 mmol/L)
   - Base deficit improving (>-2 mEq/L)

9. **When to stop MTP:**
   - Bleeding controlled
   - Normal labs (INR, platelets, fibrinogen)
   - Vital signs stable without vasopressors

## 🤰 OBSTETRIC EMERGENCIES

### PREECLAMPSIA WITH SEVERE FEATURES / ECLAMPSIA

#### Critical Thresholds for Severe Features
| Parameter | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Systolic BP | ≥160 mmHg |
| Diastolic BP | ≥110 mmHg |
| Platelets | <100,000/μL |
| Creatinine | >1.1 mg/dL or doubling |
| Liver enzymes | >2× upper limit |
| Pulmonary edema | Present |
| Cerebral symptoms | Headache, vision changes |
| Epigastric pain | Right upper quadrant pain |

#### Management Protocols

**Severe Preeclampsia:**

1. **Immediate assessment:**
   - Blood pressure in both arms
   - Deep tendon reflexes (hyperreflexia suggests worsening)
   - Urine protein (dipstick or 24-hour)
   - Labs: CBC, CMP, LFTs, PT/PTT, fibrinogen
   - Fetal monitoring

2. **Seizure prophylaxis with magnesium sulfate:**

   **Loading dose:**
   - **Magnesium sulfate 4-6 grams IV** over 15-20 minutes

   **Maintenance:**
   - **2 grams/hour continuous IV infusion**

   **Monitoring:**
   - Hourly deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)
   - Hourly urine output
   - Respiratory rate every hour
   - Magnesium level every 4-6 hours (therapeutic 4-7 mg/dL)

   **Signs of magnesium toxicity:**
   - Loss of DTRs (first sign - typically at 8-10 mg/dL)
   - Respiratory depression (at 12-15 mg/dL)
   - Cardiac arrest (at >15 mg/dL)

   **If toxic:**
   - **STOP magnesium**
   - **Calcium gluconate 1 gram IV** over 3 minutes (reversal agent)
   - Support respirations

3. **Blood pressure management:**

   **If BP ≥160/110 mmHg on two occasions 15 min apart:**
   
   **First-line agents:**
   
   **Labetalol:**
   - 20 mg IV over 2 minutes
   - If inadequate response in 10 min: 40 mg IV
   - Then 80 mg IV every 10 min (max cumulative 300 mg)
   - OR start infusion 1-2 mg/min

   **Hydralazine:**
   - 5-10 mg IV over 2 minutes
   - Repeat 5-10 mg every 20 minutes as needed
   - Max 30 mg per episode

   **Nifedipine:**
   - 10-20 mg PO immediate release
   - Repeat in 20 minutes if needed
   - Then 10-20 mg every 2-6 hours

   **Goal:**
   - Systolic BP 140-150 mmHg
   - Diastolic BP 90-100 mmHg
   - **DO NOT normalize BP** (risk placental hypoperfusion)

4. **Definitive treatment - DELIVERY:**
   - **Only cure for preeclampsia**
   
   **Timing depends on:**
   - Gestational age
   - Severity of preeclampsia
   - Maternal and fetal status

   **Indications for immediate delivery:**
   - ≥37 weeks gestation
   - Eclampsia (seizure)
   - HELLP syndrome
   - Refractory severe hypertension
   - Pulmonary edema
   - Placental abruption
   - Non-reassuring fetal status

   **If <37 weeks:**
   - Consider expectant management with close monitoring
   - Corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity if <34 weeks
   - Daily labs, BP monitoring
   - Fetal monitoring

5. **Fluid management:**
   - **Restrict fluids** (risk of pulmonary edema)
   - Total 80-100 mL/hr IV + PO intake
   - Strict intake and output
   - Goal urine output >25 mL/hr (lower than usual)
   - Foley catheter

6. **Monitor for complications:**
   - HELLP syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets)
   - Pulmonary edema
   - Acute renal failure
   - Placental abruption
   - DIC

**Eclampsia (Seizure):**

1. **During seizure:**
   - Position patient on left side
   - Protect airway (suction available)
   - Supplemental oxygen
   - **DO NOT place anything in mouth**

2. **Magnesium sulfate:**
   - **If not on magnesium:** 6 grams IV over 15 minutes, then 2 g/hr
   - **If already on magnesium:** Give additional 2-gram bolus IV

3. **If seizures recur despite magnesium:**
   - Consider **lorazepam 2-4 mg IV** or **diazepam 5-10 mg IV**
   - **Phenytoin** is NOT first-line for eclampsia

4. **Blood pressure control:**
   - Aggressive antihypertensives as above

5. **Prepare for delivery:**
   - Eclampsia is indication for delivery regardless of gestational age
   - Stabilize mother first if possible
   - Emergent C-section if cannot stabilize or fetal distress

6. **Postpartum:**
   - Continue magnesium sulfate 24 hours postpartum
   - Monitor BP closely (can worsen postpartum)
   - Seizures can occur up to 4 weeks postpartum

## 👶 PEDIATRIC VITAL SIGN EMERGENCIES

### FEVER IN YOUNG INFANTS

#### Critical Age-Based Thresholds

| Age Group | Temperature Threshold | Significance |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------|
| <28 days (neonate) | ≥38°C (100.4°F) | High risk of serious bacterial infection |
| 1-3 months | ≥38°C (100.4°F) | Requires evaluation and possible admission |
| 3-36 months | ≥39°C (102.2°F) | Risk of occult bacteremia |

#### Management Protocols

**Fever in Neonate (<28 days):**

**This is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY - high risk of sepsis, meningitis**

1. **Immediate full septic workup:**
   - **Blood culture**
   - **Urine culture** (via catheterization, NOT bag)
   - **Lumbar puncture** for CSF analysis and culture
   - **CBC with differential**
   - **CRP or procalcitonin**
   - **Chest X-ray** if respiratory symptoms

2. **Empiric antibiotics (DO NOT DELAY):**
   - **Ampicillin 50 mg/kg IV** every 8-12 hours (covers Listeria, Enterococcus)
   - PLUS **Gentamicin 4-5 mg/kg IV** once daily (or cefotaxime 50 mg/kg every 8-12 hours)
   - If concern for HSV (vesicles, seizures, CSF pleocytosis): add **acyclovir 20 mg/kg IV** every 8 hours

3. **Hospitalization:**
   - Admit ALL febrile neonates
   - IV antibiotics pending cultures
   - Close monitoring

**Fever in Infant 1-3 months:**

**Risk stratification using clinical criteria (Rochester, Philadelphia, Boston):**

**Low-risk criteria (ALL must be met):**
- Well-appearing
- No focal infection on exam
- WBC 5,000-15,000
- Absolute band count <1,500
- Normal urinalysis
- If diarrhea: <5 WBCs per high-power field in stool

**If low-risk:**
- May consider outpatient management with close follow-up
- Some protocols: give ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IM
- Recheck in 24 hours
- Return precautions to parents

**If high-risk or any red flags:**
- Full septic workup
- Empiric antibiotics: **Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV** (or ampicillin + gentamicin)
- Hospitalization

**Red flags requiring admission:**
- Ill-appearing
- Immunocompromised
- Prematurity
- Respiratory distress
- Dehydration
- Unreliable parents/poor follow-up

**Fever in Child 3-36 months:**

**Risk based on appearance and temperature:**

**Well-appearing, temp 38-39°C:**
- Examine for source
- Urinalysis if <24 months (UTI screening)
- No antibiotics if no source found
- Anticipatory guidance, close follow-up

**Well-appearing, temp ≥39°C:**
- Higher risk of occult bacteremia (though less common post-pneumococcal vaccine)
- Blood culture if toxic-appearing or concerning exam
- Urinalysis if <24 months
- Consider empiric antibiotics if unreliable follow-up

**Ill-appearing (regardless of temperature):**
- Full septic workup
- Empiric antibiotics: **Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV**
- Hospitalization

### PEDIATRIC TACHYCARDIA

#### Age-Based Heart Rate Thresholds (95th percentile)

| Age | Normal HR | Tachycardia Threshold |
|-----|-----------|----------------------|
| <1 month | 100-180 | >180 bpm |
| 1-12 months | 100-160 | >180 bpm |
| 1-2 years | 80-130 | >160 bpm |
| 3-5 years | 80-120 | >140 bpm |
| 6-12 years | 70-110 | >130 bpm |
| >12 years | 60-100 | >120 bpm |

#### Management Protocols

**Assess if child is stable or unstable:**

**Unstable signs:**
- Altered mental status
- Signs of shock (weak pulses, prolonged cap refill, hypotension)
- Hypotension
- Chest pain
- Respiratory distress

**If UNSTABLE with tachycardia:**
- **Synchronized cardioversion immediately**
- 0.5-1 J/kg, increase to 2 J/kg if needed
- Sedate if possible but don't delay
- Call for pediatric code team

**If STABLE, determine rhythm:**

**Sinus tachycardia:**
- P waves present and normal
- Rate varies with activity
- **Treat underlying cause:** fever, pain, dehydration, anxiety
- Fluid bolus 20 mL/kg if dehydrated
- Antipyretics if febrile

**Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT):**
- HR usually >220 in infants, >180 in children
- Abrupt onset/offset
- No beat-to-beat variability
- P waves absent or abnormal

**Treatment for stable SVT:**

1. **Vagal maneuvers** (if age-appropriate):
   - Ice to face (infants)
   - Valsalva, bearing down (older children)
   - Carotid massage (avoid in infants)

2. **Adenosine:**
   - **0.1 mg/kg rapid IV push** (max first dose 6 mg)
   - Immediately follow with 5-10 mL NS flush
   - If no response: **0.2 mg/kg** (max 12 mg)
   - **Document rhythm** during administration (brief asystole normal)

3. **If adenosine fails:**
   - **Synchronized cardioversion 0.5-1 J/kg**
   - Pediatric cardiology consult

**Ventricular tachycardia (wide complex):**
- **If pulseless:** Defibrillation 2 J/kg, CPR, follow PALS algorithm
- **If stable:** Amiodarone 5 mg/kg IV over 20-60 min

## 🚨 UNIVERSAL CRITICAL ESCALATION CRITERIA

**Activate rapid response/code team for ANY of the following:**

### Respiratory
- **Respiratory rate <8 or >30** breaths/min (adult)
- **SpO2 <85%** despite supplemental oxygen
- **Acute respiratory distress**
- **New requirement for non-rebreather mask or higher**

### Cardiovascular
- **Systolic BP <90 mmHg** (adult) or drop >40 from baseline
- **Heart rate <40 or >140** bpm with symptoms
- **New onset chest pain** with vital sign changes
- **New arrhythmia** with hemodynamic compromise

### Neurological
- **Acute change in mental status**
- **GCS drop ≥2 points**
- **New onset seizure**
- **Acute focal neurological deficit**

### General
- **Unresponsiveness**
- **Threatened airway**
- **Staff concern** - trust your clinical judgment

## 📞 ESCALATION PATHWAYS

**When vital signs exceed thresholds:**

1. **Immediate bedside assessment**
2. **Notify charge nurse and physician**
3. **Implement initial interventions per protocol**
4. **Increase monitoring frequency**
5. **Consider rapid response team if deteriorating**
6. **Document thoroughly**
7. **Reassess frequently**

**Always err on the side of escalating care.**

---

## 📚 KEY PRINCIPLES

1. **Context is critical** - always consider:
   - Patient's baseline vital signs
   - Trend over time (single value vs. pattern)
   - Medications affecting vital signs
   - Age and comorbidities
   - Overall clinical appearance

2. **Treat the patient, not the number**
   - A "normal" vital sign in a sick-appearing patient warrants investigation
   - Abnormal vital sign in asymptomatic patient may be baseline

3. **Time-sensitive interventions save lives:**
   - Antibiotics in sepsis (within 1 hour)
   - tPA in stroke (within 4.5 hours)
   - Reperfusion in STEMI (within 90 minutes)
   - Blood pressure control in ICH (within 1 hour)

4. **When in doubt:**
   - Increase monitoring
   - Call for help
   - Document your clinical reasoning
   - Escalate care
