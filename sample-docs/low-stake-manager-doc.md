# Team Operations Guide
**Scheduling, Planning & Documentation Standards**

---

## 📅 SCHEDULING & PLANNING

### Regular Team Meetings

#### Weekly Status Updates
**Scheduled Time:** Wednesdays at 2:00 PM  
**Duration:** 30 minutes  
**Location:** Conference Room B or via Teams

**Purpose:**
- Share project progress updates
- Identify blockers and challenges
- Coordinate upcoming work
- Quick team alignment

**Meeting Format:**
- Round-robin updates (5 minutes per person)
- Open discussion of blockers (10 minutes)
- Week-ahead preview (5 minutes)

**Note:** If 2pm doesn't work for your schedule due to client meetings or focus time needs, we can shift to 3pm. Just let the team know by Monday.

---

#### Bi-Weekly Sprint Planning
**Scheduled Time:** Every other Monday at 10:00 AM  
**Duration:** 1 hour  
**Location:** Main Conference Room

**Agenda Order:**
1. Review previous sprint outcomes (10 min)
2. Prioritize backlog items (20 min)
3. Assign tasks and commitments (20 min)
4. Questions and clarifications (10 min)

---

#### Monthly All-Hands
**Scheduled Time:** First Friday of each month at 9:00 AM  
**Duration:** 1 hour  
**Location:** Large Conference Room or Auditorium

**Standard Agenda Flow:**
1. Leadership updates and company news (15 min)
2. Department highlights and wins (20 min)
3. New team member introductions (10 min)
4. Q&A session (15 min)

*Agenda items can be reordered based on urgency or guest speaker availability.*

### Conference Room Booking Guidelines

#### Room Selection Criteria

**For Small Team Meetings (2-4 people):**
- Conference Room A (Huddle Room)
- Conference Room C (Small)
- Use any available small space

**For Medium Team Meetings (5-10 people):**
- Conference Room B (Medium)
- Conference Room D (Medium)

**For Large Meetings (11+ people):**
- Main Conference Room
- Auditorium (for 25+)

**For Client Meetings:**
- Executive Conference Room (preferred - best furnishings)
- Main Conference Room (if Executive Room unavailable)

**For Video Calls:**
- Any room with "VC Equipment" in the name
- Rooms A, B, and Main Conference Room have the best video/audio setup

#### Booking Process
- Book through Outlook Calendar or room scheduling system
- Reserve 15 minutes before/after for setup and buffer
- Cancel if meeting is rescheduled (don't be a ghost booker!)
- For recurring meetings, book the same room when possible for consistency

**Conflict Resolution:**
- Internal team meetings can flex to any available room
- Client meetings and interviews take priority
- Check with meeting organizer before bumping their reservation

### Casual Friday Implementation

#### Overview
Casual Friday allows employees to dress more comfortably while maintaining a professional environment.

#### What's Acceptable

**Appropriate Casual Attire:**
- Jeans (clean, no rips or distressing)
- Polo shirts, casual button-downs
- Blouses, casual dresses
- Sweaters, cardigans
- Khakis, chinos
- Sneakers (clean), casual shoes
- Company-branded apparel

**Business Casual Still Required:**
- If you have client meetings scheduled
- If you're presenting to external stakeholders
- During important company events

#### What's Not Acceptable

**Please Avoid:**
- Athletic wear (gym clothes, yoga pants, athletic shorts)
- Beachwear (flip-flops, tank tops, shorts)
- Clothing with offensive graphics or text
- Excessively worn or torn clothing
- Pajamas or sleepwear

#### Special Considerations

**Client-Facing Roles:**
- Check your calendar each Friday morning
- Keep a blazer or professional layer at your desk
- Default to business casual if uncertain

**Virtual Meetings:**
- Casual below the waist is fine
- Keep professional on top if you're on camera

**Department Variations:**
- Engineering/IT: More relaxed interpretation
- Sales/Client Services: Err on the business casual side
- HR/Leadership: Role model appropriate standards

#### Opt-Out Option
If you prefer to dress business casual every day, that's completely fine! This is about comfort, not conformity.

## 📄 DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS

### Internal Report Formatting

#### Font Guidelines

**Primary Body Text:**
- **Font:** Calibri, Arial, or Times New Roman
- **Size:** 11pt or 12pt (your choice - both are perfectly readable)
- **Line Spacing:** 1.15 or 1.5
- **Color:** Black or dark gray

**Headings:**
- **Main Heading (H1):** 14pt or 16pt, Bold
- **Subheading (H2):** 12pt or 14pt, Bold
- **Subheading (H3):** 11pt or 12pt, Bold

**Emphasis:**
- Use **bold** for important points
- Use *italics* for definitions or emphasis
- Use highlights sparingly (yellow highlight for action items is fine)

**The Key Principle:** As long as your document is easy to read and looks professional, the exact font size within this range is up to you. Consistency within a document matters more than the specific choice.

#### Margins and Layout
- **Margins:** 1 inch on all sides (default Word setting is fine)
- **Paragraphs:** Either indent first line OR add space between paragraphs (not both)
- **Page Numbers:** Bottom center or top right
- **Headers:** Include document title and date

---

### File Naming Conventions

#### Standard Format

**For Project Documents:**
```
[Project Name]_[Document Type]_[Date]_[Version]
```

**Examples:**
- `Website_Redesign_Proposal_2024-01-15_v1`
- `Q4_Marketing_Plan_2024-10-01_v2`
- `Client_Onboarding_Process_2024-03-20_Final`

**For Meeting Notes:**
```
[Meeting Type]_[Date]_[Topic]
```

**Examples:**
- `Team_Status_2024-01-17_Sprint_Planning`
- `Client_Meeting_2024-02-01_Acme_Corp`
- `AllHands_2024-01-05_Notes`

#### Date Format
- Use YYYY-MM-DD format for sorting (e.g., 2024-01-17)
- This ensures files sort chronologically

#### Version Control
- `v1`, `v2`, `v3` for drafts in progress
- `Final` for completed versions
- `Final_v2` if you have to revise a "final" version (we've all been there)

#### General Principles

**Do:**
- Use underscores (_) to separate elements
- Keep names descriptive but concise
- Be consistent within your team/project
- Include dates for time-sensitive documents

**Avoid:**
- Special characters (!, @, #, $, %, etc.)
- Spaces (use underscores instead)
- Vague names like "Document1" or "Final_FINAL_version2"
- Excessively long names (keep under 50 characters if possible)

**The Flexibility:**
If your team has a different naming convention that works well and everyone understands it, stick with it! The goal is findability and clarity, not rigid standardization.

### List Formatting in Internal Documents

#### When to Use Bullet Points vs. Numbered Lists

**Use Bullet Points (•) When:**
- Order doesn't matter
- Listing features, benefits, or items
- The items are of equal importance
- Examples:
  - Team members
  - Project deliverables
  - Meeting attendees
  - Brainstorming ideas

**Use Numbered Lists (1, 2, 3) When:**
- Order or sequence matters
- Following steps in a process
- Ranking or prioritizing items
- Referencing specific items later in the document
- Examples:
  - Installation instructions
  - Event schedule
  - Top 5 priorities
  - Step-by-step procedures

**Use Checkboxes (☐) When:**
- Creating action items
- Task lists
- Tracking completion status

#### Formatting Style Options

**Simple Bullets:**
- Clean and minimal
- Good for short lists
- Easy to scan

**Bullets with Bold Headers:**
- **Project Alpha:** Description of project status
- **Budget Review:** Summary of financial standing
- **Team Updates:** Personnel changes and news

**Multi-level Lists:**
- Main category
  - Sub-item one
  - Sub-item two
    - Detail point
- Next main category

**Choose the style that makes your content clearest.** There's no single "correct" format - readability is what matters.

### Color Coding Systems

#### When Color Coding is Helpful

**Good Uses of Color:**
- **Status indicators** in project trackers (Red = Behind, Yellow = At Risk, Green = On Track)
- **Priority levels** in task lists (High, Medium, Low)
- **Department identification** in shared calendars
- **Version highlighting** in comparison documents
- **Category organization** in spreadsheets

#### Suggested Color Schemes

**Status Tracking:**
- 🔴 **Red:** Critical, Overdue, Blocked, High Priority
- 🟡 **Yellow:** At Risk, Warning, Medium Priority
- 🟢 **Green:** On Track, Completed, Low Priority
- 🔵 **Blue:** Information, Reference, Future
- ⚪ **Gray:** Inactive, Archived, On Hold

**Department Colors (example):**
- **Marketing:** Purple
- **Sales:** Blue
- **Engineering:** Green
- **HR:** Orange
- **Finance:** Red

#### Best Practices

**Do:**
- Create a color key/legend if using multiple colors
- Use consistent colors within a document or system
- Ensure colors have enough contrast for readability
- Consider colorblind accessibility (don't rely on color alone)

**Don't:**
- Use too many colors (limit to 4-6 in most documents)
- Use color as the ONLY indicator (also use text labels, icons, or patterns)
- Use very bright or neon colors (hard on the eyes)
- Assume everyone will print in color

**The Bottom Line:** Color coding is a tool to enhance understanding, not a requirement. If your document is perfectly clear in black and white, you don't need to add colors just for decoration.

### PowerPoint Template Choices

#### Available Templates

**For Internal Team Presentations:**

**Option 1: Simple White Background**
- Clean and minimal
- Easy to read
- Fast to create
- Good for data-heavy presentations
- **Use when:** Quick updates, internal reviews, working sessions

**Option 2: Company-Branded Template**
- Includes logo and brand colors
- More polished look
- Professional appearance
- **Use when:** Department presentations, all-hands meetings, cross-team sharing

**Option 3: Dark Background Template**
- Modern aesthetic
- Good for large-screen presentations
- Reduces eye strain in dark rooms
- **Use when:** Auditorium presentations, video recordings, creative pitches

#### Template Elements

**Standard Slide Layouts Available:**
- Title slide
- Title and content
- Two-column layout
- Image with caption
- Quote or callout
- Section divider
- Thank you / Questions slide

#### Customization Guidelines

**You Can:**
- Change slide layouts as needed
- Adjust text sizes for readability
- Add your own images and graphics
- Remove elements that don't serve your content
- Use different templates for different sections

**Please Keep:**
- Company logo (on title slide at minimum)
- Consistent font family throughout
- Readable text sizes (minimum 18pt for body text)

#### Font Sizes for Presentations

**Recommended Sizes:**
- **Title:** 36-44pt
- **Headings:** 28-32pt
- **Body Text:** 18-24pt
- **Captions:** 14-16pt (minimum)

**The 6-6 Rule (optional guideline):**
- No more than 6 bullet points per slide
- No more than 6 words per bullet
- Use more slides rather than cramming content

#### Design Tips

**Keep It Simple:**
- One main idea per slide
- Plenty of white space
- High contrast between text and background
- Consistent alignment

**Use Visuals:**
- Replace text with images or diagrams when possible
- Use charts for data (not tables of numbers)
- Icons can replace bullet points
- Screenshots for demonstrations

**The Flexibility:**
Choose the template that fits your audience and purpose. Internal brainstorming session? Simple white background is perfect. Presenting to leadership? Use the branded template. No template is "wrong" as long as your content is clear and professional.

## 🎯 GENERAL PRINCIPLES

### Why These Are Low-Stake Decisions

These guidelines are **recommendations, not rigid rules** because:

1. **Different contexts need different approaches** - A quick team sync doesn't need the same formality as a board presentation

2. **Personal preferences vary** - Some people think better with bullet points, others with numbered lists

3. **Tools evolve** - File naming conventions that work today might change with new software

4. **Teams should have autonomy** - If your team has a better system that works for you, use it!

5. **Perfectionism slows progress** - Spending 10 minutes choosing between 11pt and 12pt font is not productive

### When to Follow Guidelines Closely

**Be more careful with:**
- External-facing documents (client proposals, marketing materials)
- Legal or compliance documents (follow exact templates)
- Cross-department collaboration (shared standards help)
- Documents going to leadership or board

### When to Adapt Freely

**Feel free to adjust for:**
- Personal working documents
- Small team internal use
- Brainstorming and ideation
- Quick updates and informal communication

## 💡 QUICK DECISION FRAMEWORK

**When facing a formatting or scheduling choice, ask:**

1. **Is it readable/understandable?** → If yes, you're good
2. **Is anyone likely to be confused?** → If no, proceed
3. **Will this decision matter in a week?** → Probably not for these items
4. **Does it align generally with team norms?** → Close enough is fine

**If you answered positively to these questions, stop deliberating and move forward with your work!**
